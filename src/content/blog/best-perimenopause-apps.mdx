# Best Perimenopause Tracker Apps in 2026 (Honest Comparison)

Searching for a perimenopause tracking app returns a wide range of options, from general period trackers to community platforms to dedicated symptom logs. Not all of them are well-suited to what most perimenopausal women actually need: a reliable way to log symptoms daily, identify patterns, and bring useful data to a doctor. This comparison covers the main options and is direct about what each does and doesn't do well.

## What to Look for in a Perimenopause App

Before comparing specific apps, it helps to clarify what makes a tracking app genuinely useful during perimenopause — as opposed to useful for general period tracking or reproductive health.

The most important features for perimenopause specifically are: daily symptom logging across a wide range of symptoms (not just cycle data), severity ratings rather than just presence, cycle tracking that handles irregular periods well, pattern analysis over weeks and months, and — critically — the ability to export or share data with a healthcare provider. Apple Health integration is a meaningful bonus, since it can add sleep and heart rate context without manual entry.

For an overview of what to actually track and why, see [How to Track Perimenopause Symptoms (And Why It Actually Helps)](/blog/how-to-track-perimenopause-symptoms).

## The Main Options in 2026

### Peri Tracker

**Best for: Daily symptom tracking and doctor visit preparation.**

Peri Tracker is built specifically for perimenopause — not adapted from a period tracker or a general wellness app. The core experience is a daily log covering the symptoms most relevant to the transition: hot flashes (with time of day and severity), sleep quality, mood, energy, brain fog, cycle data, and custom symptoms. Severity ratings are built in, not optional.

The standout feature is the exportable doctor report: a structured PDF covering your cycle history, top symptoms by frequency and severity, and trend data over the last 1–3 months. This is designed to be handed directly to a gynecologist or GP. Most competing apps do not offer this in a format clinicians find immediately readable.

Data is stored locally on your device (not sent to third-party servers), which addresses the privacy concerns many women have about sharing health data with external platforms. Apple Health integration means sleep data flows in automatically if you wear an Apple Watch.

Pricing: free trial, then $2.99/month or $24.99/year.

### Balance (Newson Health)

**Best for: Educational content and community.**

Balance is the most downloaded menopause app in the UK and has a large, active user community. It was developed with input from Dr. Louise Newson, a specialist in menopause medicine, and the educational content — articles, guides, expert Q&As — is high quality.

The symptom tracking in Balance is functional but less granular than a dedicated tracker. The emphasis is on content consumption and community rather than clinical data. If you want to understand perimenopause and connect with others going through it, Balance is excellent. If you want a detailed daily log you can show your doctor, it is less suited to that purpose.

Pricing: free with premium subscription option.

### Clue

**Best for: Cycle tracking with some symptom logging.**

Clue is a general period and cycle tracker with a well-designed interface and good cycle prediction. It has added some menopause-related features, but the app's design assumptions reflect its reproductive health origins — it is built around cycle regularity that perimenopause disrupts by definition.

Clue works well for women in early perimenopause whose cycles are still relatively predictable. For women experiencing significant irregularity, the cycle-centric interface becomes less useful. Symptom logging is present but limited in the range of symptoms relevant to perimenopause.

Pricing: free with premium subscription.

### Flo

**Best for: General period tracking; limited for perimenopause specifically.**

Flo is one of the most-used period tracking apps globally. It has recently added perimenopause-related content and features, but the core product is built for reproductive-age tracking rather than the perimenopausal transition. Women in perimenopause often find the interface's emphasis on fertility and reproductive health either irrelevant or confusing.

If you are in early perimenopause and want to continue using an app you already know, Flo is a reasonable choice for cycle tracking. For dedicated perimenopause symptom logging, it is not the most efficient tool.

Pricing: free with premium subscription.

### Evia

**Best for: Hot flash and anxiety reduction via hypnotherapy.**

Evia is an outlier in this category — it is not a symptom tracker but a hypnotherapy app for menopause symptoms, with a focus on reducing hot flash frequency and anxiety. It is backed by a small but legitimate clinical evidence base. At roughly $150+ for lifetime access, it is premium-priced, but the revenue data suggests a dedicated user base willing to pay for results.

Worth considering as a complement to a symptom tracker, not a replacement.

## Which One Should You Choose?

If your priority is tracking symptoms consistently and being able to show your doctor useful data, Peri Tracker is designed specifically for that workflow.

If your priority is understanding perimenopause — reading expert content, hearing other women's experiences, learning what to expect — Balance is the strongest option for that.

If you want to keep using a period tracker you already know and your symptoms are still relatively mild, Clue or Flo can serve you, with the caveat that their perimenopausal features are less developed.

The best approach for many women is a combination: Peri Tracker for daily logging and doctor visits, Balance for education and community. Both are available on a free trial.
